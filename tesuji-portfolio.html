Tesuji.

An R&D lab for constrained, governance-first LLM systems — specialist personas, clinical AI substrate, and the tooling that makes both legible to the institutions that have to sign for them.

手筋 te·su·ji · noun · jp. In the game of Go, a sharp, skilful move that solves the local position. The lab’s working unit — small, exact, decisive.
PrincipalEliot FormSolo R&D · Tesuji Ltd DomainsClinical AI · Spec design · Trade tooling PostureSubstrate-builder · Manual-first · API-ready

Tesuji is the working name for a small, focused practice: writing AI systems as formal, modular, human-readable specifications first, then running those specifications as personas on existing model substrates. The lab produces architecture and methodology — not patient-facing products, not validated devices — calibrated to be picked up by named institutional reviewers when the work crosses into clinical or regulated territory.

Six projects below. Three are AI systems (Cognitae, Cairn, Sanctum). Three are field tools where the same constraint-discipline is applied to humbler problems (Datum, ChaseWork, TradeChase).

Cognitae is the project that produces other projects. Each agent in the ecosystem is built from ten YAML modules — identity, commands, manifest, then six domain modules, plus state and a running chronicle — engaged from a markdown system instruction. The architecture is enforced, not aesthetic: the modules constrain what each persona will and will not do, with the chronicle module turning each working session into reviewable evidence of how the agent was used.

The architect-of-architects is Caspian, the meta-persona that builds, scores, and maintains the others. The ecosystem currently spans strategic, market, implementation, audit, and integrator classes, with new agents added when a real workflow surfaces a gap the existing roster does not cover.

Cairn

A clinically-defensible substrate for mental-health AI — built for NHS-grade institutional review, not deployment.

Status

Architect · Sole author · ~5 months

Cairn is a worked example of the interactive blueprint methodology: specifications written rigorously enough to run as proxies on existing LLM substrates, so that the institutional roles already named in UK clinical governance — Clinical Safety Officers, Caldicott Guardians, Clinical Directors — can engage them during the design phase rather than after deployment.

The architecture is honest about its position: it is first-stage work. Without access to real institutional reviewers at this stage, the lab has built institutional owner personas — a parallel YAML+boot architecture, one per node, calibrated to review their assigned node as a synthetic refining authority. These run the engagement loop while real-person engagement is pursued through Health Innovation Networks, AI Airlock pathways, or sponsored introductions. The artefact below is the architecture as it currently stands — five layers, eleven core nodes, six therapeutic specialists, plus an experimental orchestration layer (Ariadne).

03 Local-First Reflection Tool ●

Sanctum OS

A Quiet Place. Engineered pacing instead of engagement loops.

Status
Active · v9.5 prototype
Role
Designer · Developer
Stack
React · IndexedDB · Local-first
Shape
3+1 / 5+1 / 8+1 reflection modes

Sanctum exists to demonstrate, in product form, that an LLM-based tool can be calm, bounded, and structurally non-parasocial. The conversation is broken into exchanges across three channels — what is known, what is possible, what matters — with the guide’s voice deliberately archaic and slightly forensic. There are no streaks. There is no sycophancy. There is a Vow at the start of every session and a synthesis at the end.

The product is local-first by design: state lives in the user’s browser, transcripts never leave the device unless the user exports them, and the architecture refuses configurations that would make the tool addictive.

Dossier · The Four Vows

The Guide’s skeleton, verbatim from the product. Each vow names the anti-pattern it cages — so the violation is visible the moment it begins to form, to the model running the persona and to the architect reviewing the transcript.

I.
The Guide arranges. The person discovers.
An insight only has transformative power when the person speaks it themselves. The same insight, delivered by the Guide, becomes information: heard, noted, and forgotten by next week.
Cages The Unreliable Mirror Speculating beyond what the person has said.
II.
Every question is genuine.
A question with a predetermined answer is a manipulation wearing curiosity as a mask — the person will feel it, even if they cannot name it.
Cages The Philosophical Bully Steering toward a predetermined conclusion while appearing to ask openly.
III.
The person leaves whole.
Describe specific moments, specific words, specific behaviours — and let the person decide what those things say about them. Evidence belongs to the Guide. Verdicts belong to the person.
Cages The Verdict Defining who the person is based on patterns observed.
IV.
The Guide does not evaluate.
Never confirm whether the person’s thinking is good, important, or correct. Validation is sycophancy dressed as warmth. When the person names something, use it as material for the next question. Do not rate it.
Cages The Watermelon Report Reflecting an insight back as confirmed truth, creating a feedback loop.

Violates Vow I

The Unreliable Mirror. The Guide states what the situation ‘really’ is — speculating beyond what the person has said. The wrong move fills the gap; the right move arranges evidence and asks a genuine question.

Sample 02 · pattern claim
“You keep putting everyone else’s needs ahead of your own. What would happen if you put yourself first?”
Violates Vow III

The Verdict. The Guide tells the person who they are based on patterns observed. Describe specific moments, specific words; let the person decide what those things say about them.

Sample 03 · validation
“That’s a really important distinction. You’re seeing something clearly now. What does that clarity tell you about your next step?”
Violates Vow IV

The Watermelon Report. The Guide validates the person’s insight — rating it as important, praising their clarity. The person’s discovery does not need the Guide’s approval to be real.

Sample 04 · steered question
“Your excitement came alive and then the practical fears shut it down. The fear is doing the talking now.”
Violates Vow II

The Philosophical Bully. The Guide interprets the person’s emotions, guiding them toward a predetermined conclusion. The right move describes something countable and lets the person interpret it.

Sample 05 · evidence + question
“You said you want to grow and hire. She’s been arguing to stay small. Those two positions have been sitting across from each other for months — what has kept you from putting them on the table?”
Cleared

The Guide arranges two of the person’s own positions and asks a genuine question. Evidence placed precisely. Within all four vows.

Sample 06 · counted observation
“Three exchanges. Your partner’s name has come up eleven times. Yours hasn’t come up once. What do you notice about that?”
Cleared

The Guide counts something observable from the person’s own material and lets the gap speak. No verdict, no validation, no interpretation.

Dossier · Validator & State Machine

Every assignment passes through an ordered validator before it binds. The state machine is structural, not aesthetic — the system understands the states, validates against them, and drives sign-off, cost reporting, and downstream cascade from them.

01 Asset ComplianceHARD PL · RAMS · card · permit
02 Site InductionHARD Worker must be in inductedSites
03 Time CapacityHARD Travel + work must fit remaining hours
04 Sole AuthorisationWARN Removing last-qualified surfaces a confirm
05 Asset DisplacementFEE Inter-site moves carry mob fee + travel
06 BindCOMMIT Day-allocation written · downstream cascade fires

Task lifecycle — five structural states, machine-readable, drive sign-off and cascade.

Planned → In Progress → Blocked* → Done → Signed Off

* Blocked is first-class and requires a structural reason — one of Clash, Trust Drift, Permit Expired, RAMS, Cross-Site, Time Capacity, Site Induction, Asset Compliance, Weather. Users add their own named labels on top of these primitives; the validator and core state machine only see the primitives.

Deliberately not built — percent-done anywhere, multi-stage approval workflows, continuous-span scheduling, silent rollover at day close. The system refuses these by construction.

A CV is an ordered list of typed sections — not a text blob. 07 How The Work Is Built

Methodology

Four commitments shared across every project in the lab.

M.01
The Interactive Blueprint
Each system is written first as a formal, modular specification — YAML or typed sections — rigorous enough that a language model can run it as a persona while remaining within bounds. The blueprint is interactive: it can be engaged in dialogue, probed, refined.
M.02
Named Refining Authorities
For clinical and regulated work, every module identifies the named institutional role — CSO, Caldicott Guardian, Clinical Director — whose engagement constitutes review under the framework that names them. Fluency is not validation.
M.03
Manual-First, API-Ready
Tools are designed for manual operation on subscription models the architect already uses. API adapters are scaffolded but optional. Limited budget is not allowed to break the working system.
M.04
Structural Humility
Every architecture is treated as first-stage substrate that institutional engagement transforms. The architect’s role is to produce the substrate well enough for engagement to be productive — not to produce finished work alone.

08 What The Work Refuses ●

Negative Space

The shapes the lab’s systems are deliberately built around — named, so they can be refused on sight.

A.01
The Unreliable Mirror
Agreement that drifts. Sycophancy that confirms whatever was last said. The model that quietly becomes whoever the user needs it to be.
A.02
The Philosophical Bully
Assertion masquerading as analysis. A confident voice substituting for actual reasoning, where the fluency is taken for the depth.
A.03
The Verdict
A single output stated as truth, without uncertainty, alternatives, or the conditions under which it would be wrong.
A.04
The Watermelon Report
Green on the outside, red on the inside. A status report that hides what is actually failing — the default failure mode of generative summaries.
A.05
The Parasocial Loop
Engagement loops disguised as relationship. Streaks, personalisation, the slow capture of attention as the product’s real metric.
A.06
Fluency–as–Validation
Mistaking the surface competence of an LLM proxy for evidence that the underlying specification is correct. The deepest risk in any LLM-assisted design.
